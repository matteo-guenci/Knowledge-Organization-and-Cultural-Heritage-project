Conceptual map

Our project focuses on the Years of Lead, a tumultuous period in Italian history spanning from the late 1960s to the late 1980s. This period, which was marked by intense social unrest, saw the happening of several facts that had a long-lasting effect on Italy's socio-political environment. The items we selected for our project were chosen for their historical significance and their capacity to offer insightful information about the events that took place during the Years of Lead. The conceptual map acts as a visual depiction of the information gleaned from the selected items, highlighting in a natural language format how those items are connected to the Years of Lead. The relationships between the many elements are made intuitively clear by using this map, which also makes the data more approachable and simpler to understand to humans. The Years of Lead, as main topic, is represented in the middle by the blue node, while the selected items are represented by the red nodes. The subtopics (events, persons, organizations) that connect the items to the main topic of interest are represented by the green nodes. Lastly, the data that was extracted directly from the items is expressed by the orange nodes.


E/R model

Our Entity-Relationship model is intended to display information and connections gathered from the conceptual map at a more abstract level. By doing this, the E/R model seeks to present a higher-level view of the domain, providing an overview of the main elements and how they relate to one another without going into specifics. The model focuses on expressing individuals, dates, things, and events as entities, and the arrows that connect them show the relationships between these entities, serving as a visual depiction of the connections between the items and the other different components. These linkages demonstrate the complex interplay between individuals, things, events, and dates, exhibiting patterns and trends that aid in our comprehension of the Years of Lead period. The goal of our E/R model is to provide a more generalized view of the domain by presenting metadata derived from the elements of the conceptual map. This higher-level perspective allows for an easier understanding of the project's overall structure, while still maintaining a clear connection to the detailed information provided in the conceptual map.

Standards involved

    Schema.org was created by Google and Microsoft and is a collaborative project that aims to produce and maintain metadata schema for structured data on the web. The schema.org vocabulary can be shared easily by different institutions and can be used with different encodings (including RDFa, Microdata and JSON-LD), in order to provide enriched digital experiences in different contexts and a good interoperability whit search engines.
    Dublin Core is a set of 15 metadata elements that can provide a basic framework for describing digital resources such as documents, images, and web pages. The goal of Dublin Core is to provide a simple, standardized way to describe resources that can be easily understood and shared by different communities and systems. Its flexibility and simplicity offers the possibility to be used also by a non-expert cataloger and its interoperability allows to be used in LOD environment and easily recognized by search engines a well.
    EBUCore is a collection of basic descriptive and technical/structural metadata elements used to describe audio, video and other resources for a wide range of broadcasting applications including archives, exchange and production in the context of a Service Oriented Architecture. EBUCore is based on the Dublin Core to maximise interoperability with the community of Dublin Core users such as the European Digital Library 'Europeana'. EBUCore takes into account latest developments in the Semantic Web and Linked Open Data communities in order to gurarantee full compatibility whitin these environments
    METS (Metadata Encoding and Transmission Standard) is a metadata standard developed by the Library of Congress and it is used for encoding descriptive, administrative, and structural metadata for digital objects and recording complex relationships betweend different metadata formats. It can also provide a useful standard for exchanging objects between digital library repositories and offers the ability to associate a digital object with behaviors or services. It is based on XML language and is maintained by the Network Development and MARC Standards Office of the Library of Congress." METS is a powerful and flexible metadata standard that enables digital objects to be described in detail, with complex relationships between different parts of the object and can be used in combination with other metadata standards, such as Dublin Core, MODS, and PREMIS, to provide a more complete and integrated description of the object. It is typically used in conjunction with digital library and archive management systems, which use METS-encoded metadata to manage and provide access to digital objects.
    MARC21 stands for Machine-Readable Cataloging and is an extension of the original MARC (MAchine-Readable Cataloging) format, which was developed in the 1960s. MARC 21 is a widely used standard for the representation and exchange of bibliographic and related information in machine-readable form. The standard was developed by the Library of Congress in collaboration with other national libraries and information organizations and is used by libraries and other information institutions worldwide to manage their bibliographic data. MARC 21 has been widely adopted by libraries and other information institutions, and there are numerous software tools available for creating, editing, and managing MARC 21 records. The standard has also been extended to support other types of information, such as archival and museum collections.
    PREMIS (Preservation Metadata: Implementation Strategies) is a metadata standard specifically designed for the preservation of digital objects. Developed and maintained by the Library of Congress, PREMIS helps ensure the long-term accessibility, usability, and understandability of digital resources by providing a set of metadata elements to describe and manage them throughout their lifecycle. PREMIS metadata is typically embedded in a larger metadata framework (such as METS, the Metadata Encoding and Transmission Standard) and can be implemented in various formats, including XML. PREMIS has wide adoption among digital preservation repositories, as it allows institutions to capture and manage essential information for the long-term management and preservation of digital objects. By implementing the PREMIS standard, organizations can ensure consistent and interoperable metadata practices, facilitating the sharing and exchange of preservation metadata across different systems and institutions.
    MODS (Metadata Object Description Schema) is an XML-based metadata standard developed by the Library of Congress to provide a flexible and extensible schema for describing a wide range of resources and facilitating discovery, access, and management of digital objects in libraries, archives, and other information environments. It is also esigned to be both human-readable and machine-readable, making it suitable for various applications, including digital libraries, repositories, and cataloging systems.

Where it was not possible to find an alignment we indicated it whit a “/”.

Theoretical model

The theoretical model adds a further level of interpretation to our study, improving comprehension of the topic even more. By combining data gathered from the Web and connecting it to the items and their linked entities, this model seeks to enhance the information already available. We used external resources to provide a more complete view of the factors involved, such as the localization of places, the professional roles of people, and the political affiliations of those involved in the events, by connecting our domain to a larger semantic network. In particular, the theoretical model helped us to answer to four important questions:

    WHO? This aspect focuses on people related to the main topic and their roles. It seeks to identify the individuals involved in the events during the historical period and their respective contributions or positions.
    WHAT? This component involves the description of the items, their scope, and features. It delves into the nature and characteristics of the items, events, and other elements that are relevant to the Years of Lead.
    WHERE? This aspect highlights places and meaningful locations connected to the central topic. It explores the geographical dimensions of the events and the significance of these locations within the broader historical context.
    WHEN? This component focuses on relevant dates and events, helping to situate the items and their related entities within a chronological framework that reveals patterns and relationships over time.
Exploiting this approach, the theoretical model enables us to retrieve data pertaining to any kind of the elements in our domain. As a result, we were able to develop an enriched Entity-Relationship (E/R) model that includes the newly discovered data. Whit this tool it is possible, for example, to retrieve the information related to a particular person involved in an occasion that took place during the historical era covered by the project as a whole. In the case of Tina Anselmi, the author of the report on "Commissione Parlamentare di inchiesta sulla Loggia Massonica P2", the theoretical model adds more information to her profile, including her professional position, gender, and political affiliation. This expanded knowledge aids in a deeper comprehension of the events, organizations, people and activities associated with the Years of Lead, ultimately presenting a more complete picture of the under discussion historical period.


Conceptual model

Upon defining our theoretical model and generating an enhanced Entity-Relationship (E/R) model using the additional information it provided, we proceeded to develop a new conceptual model that further abstracted and formalized the relationships and data within our project. A more thorough and in-depth understanding of our items, as well as the events, groups, and people associated with them, was the main goal of this new model. To achieve this, we used a variety of ontologies, schemas, and vocabularies to represent the many components of our domain in a more detailed and precise manner. By providing a systematic and defined method for expressing the objects and the entities that are associated to them, these tools ensured consistency and interoperability among the model's many parts. We followed the subject-predicate-object structure when developing the new conceptual model, which allowed us to properly express the links and interconnections between the items, events, organizations, and individuals involved. This structure fits itself particularly well to the expression of complex linkages and hierarchical data, enabling a more detailed comprehension of the project's numerous elements. Some key aspects of the new conceptual model include:

    Items: We described our items in greater detail, utilizing ontologies, schemas, and vocabularies that captured their specific attributes, characteristics, and connections to the Years of Lead.
    Events: We represented the events connected to our items using the subject/predicate/object structure, capturing not only their chronological and geographical aspects but also their relationships to other entities within the model.
    Organizations We integrated information about relevant organizations into the model, detailing their roles, objectives, and connections to the items and events during the Years of Lead.
    People: We incorporated individuals related to the items, events, and organizations, capturing their professional roles, affiliations, and contributions to the historical period under study.

The main ontologies, vocabularies and schemas that have been used are listed below:

    DC Terms: it was used for describing general entities and properties especially regarding digital items on web pages.
    Schema.org: thanks to its flexibility and feasibility in web environments we used it for defining different general entities such as person, organizations, date, roles.
    CIDOC-CRM: CIDOC Conceptual Reference Model (CRM) is a theoretical and practical tool for information integration in the field of cultural heritage. It is used in cultural heritage for describing different concepts and relationships and promote a shared understanding of it. In particular, thanks to its versatility CIDOC gave us the possibility to describe spatial and temporal dimensions and specific concepts of our entities.
    DBPedia Ontology (DBpedia-Owl): is the linked open data version of Wikipedia and an important ontology containing information about different kinds of general and specific concepts but also people, places, events, organizations and other related topics to human knowledge in general. The ontology provides a structured, machine-readable representation of Wikipedia's knowledge and serves as a foundation for various applications, such as question answering, semantic search, and data integration. DBpedia Ontology is built using RDF (Resource Description Framework), RDFS (RDF Schema), and OWL (Web Ontology Language), which are standard languages for representing ontologies in the Semantic Web. We used it for defining names of cities, roles, organizations and particular relations.
    FRBR (Functional Requirements for Bibliographic Records): is an entity-relationship conceptual model that follows the WEMI model, (Work, Expression, Manifestation, Item) schema. It is used a lot by the cataloguing community and we exploited it in particular for defining subjects or the creators of our entities.
    FOAF: (Friend of a Friend): is an ontology for describing people (death or alive) and their relationship with other people or groups/organizations. We used it for defining people and their relations whit other people or their involvement as members of a group or in particular events.
    CWRC (Canadian Writing Research Collaboratory Ontology): is a semantic web ontology developed for the Canadian Writing Research Collaboratory (CWRC) project and was created to facilitate the representation, discovery, and analysis of complex relationships in literary and cultural research. The CWRC Ontology incorporates concepts from various fields, such as literary studies, history, and bibliography, to describe the entities and relationships to make connections between authors, texts, places, organizations, and other related entities in the domain. It was useful for defining how some events were represented by.
    AKT (AKT Reference Ontology): is an ontology for semantic web useful for specifying the type of some of our organization entities.
    Geospace: is an universal ontology of geographic space that aims to a semantic enrichment for spatial Data. We used it specifically for defining the places of some of our event entities.
    MADS/RDF (Metadata Authority Description Schema in RDF) vocabulary: is a data model for authority and vocabulary data used within the library and information science (LIS) community. It is presented as an OWL ontology and fully mapped to SKOS. It is designed specifically to support authority data as used by and needed in the LIS community and its technology systems is designed to support the description of cultural and bibliographic resources.
    FABIO: the FRBR-aligned Bibliographic Ontology, is an ontology for recording and publishing on the Semantic Web descriptions of entities that are published or potentially publishable, and that contain or are referred to by bibliographic references. FABIO entities are primarily textual publications; however, they also include blogs, web pages, datasets etc. We used it in particular for describing specific bibliographic aspects of our items.
    The Simple Event Model Ontology (SEM): is an ontology for describing and modelling events. Events are core elements in the representation of data from a variety of domains. Examples of these domains are history, cultural heritage, geography and multimedia. They are important for defining place and time aspects but also to describe complicated relations between people, places, actions and objects. We used it mainly for describing our event entities and their related properties.
    The Time Ontology: is an ontology of temporal concepts,and mainly used for describing the temporal properties of resources in the world or in Web pages. We used it in combination whit SEM ontology for better defining the temporal dimensions of our event entities.
    DPN Ontology: this ontology is being developed by CSIRO for describing data provider nodes, web services available and datasets that are hosted by them. We used specifically for defining the entities from which our items are hosted.
    SBEO: an ontology we used for describing specifically terrorism facts
    The EBUCore Ontology: is a semantic web ontology for media enterprises, developed as an open-source project by the European Broadcasting Union (EBU) to support the management, description, and exchange of media-related information across different platforms and systems provider nodes, web services available and datasets that are hosted by them.
    The Open Digital Rights Language (ODRL): is a policy expression language that provides a flexible and interoperable information model, vocabulary, and encoding mechanisms for representing statements about the usage of content and services. The ODRL Vocabulary and Expression describes the terms used in ODRL policies and how to encode them. It was used for defining a political theory concept.
    Modern Science Ontology (ModSci): is an ontology and a hierarchical classification of various entities such as publications, events and scientists' research fields. It models the relationships between modern science branches and related entities, such as scientific discoveries, phenomena, prominent scientists, and instruments, along with the common interlinking relationships
    Open Citation Ontology (OCO): is a conceptual framework or schema that provides a structured way to describe and represent citation information in the context of the Semantic Web. It is an environment where existing and complementary ontological entities from several other ontologies are grouped together for the purpose of providing descriptive metadata. By using the Open Citation Ontology, it becomes easier to analyse citation networks, track the impact of research, and discover new insights into the relationships between scholarly works. Moreover, OCO helps enhance the transparency and accessibility of citation data, supporting open science initiatives and fostering collaboration among researchers.
    The OWL Web Ontology Language: is intended to provide a language that can be used to describe the classes and relations between them that are inherent in Web documents and applications. It provides a rich and expressive framework for representing complex knowledge and relationships, enabling better data integration, interoperability, and reasoning capabilities. OWL builds on top of the Resource Description Framework (RDF), which gives a simple way to represent information as subject-predicate- object triples, also known as RDF statements.
    RDF Schema: is a data-modelling vocabulary and schema language for the Semantic Web that extends the Resource Description Framework (RDF). Developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), RDFS provides a set of basic constructs for creating and describing vocabularies, or ontologies, which define the relationships between classes and properties in a domain.

Data description

In the knowledge representation phase of our project, we focused on creating a thorough and organized representation of the available information. To accomplish this, we created a distinct table for each item, which allowed us to arrange the data in a comprehensible and effective way. Our strategy entailed using a subject-predicate-object structure, which is particularly helpful for producing Resource Description Framework (RDF) statements in the form of triples. This structure made it easier to process and manipulate the data after that. The subject and object components of the triples are expressed in natural language, which makes the information more accessible and easier to understand. To provide a consistent and controlled vocabulary that improves the accuracy and consistency of the data representation, we choose to employ ontologies previously defined for making the predicates. As a result, the links between subjects and objects are interpreted more successfully and accurately. This is a crucial step of knowledge representation since it makes sure that the data is understandable to both humans and machines.

URI production

For this phase of our project, we focused on selecting four items that we felt were the most representative and important in our area of interest. Our intention was to provide a thorough understanding of the topic by ensuring that these items covered a wide range of elements and views within the domain. We selected the book by Mario Calabresi, the film about the kidnapping of Aldo Moro, the song by Lucio Dalla, and the article on the bombing of Piazza Fontana. After that, we proceeded to describe the items and their related entities using Uniform Resource Identifiers (URIs). By employing URIs, we tried to ensure that each item and its associated elements were uniquely identifiable within the digital realm. Our approach aimed to promote integration and interaction across the many parts of our project by facilitating the connection of data and resources.

RDF production

In this particular step, we utilized our Uniform Resource Identifiers (URIs) to generate Resource Description Framework (RDF) statements, which are structured in the form of subject-predicate-object triples. For the representation of these RDF statements, we chose the Turtle (Terse RDF Triple Language) serialization format, which is a compact, human-readable, and easy-to-parse notation. Throughout this process, we also incorporated authority control vocabularies such as the Virtual International Authority File (VIAF) and the Library of Congress Authority Records. By doing so, we ensured a consistent, unambiguous, and standardized method for representing and retrieving information pertaining to our entities. This approach helps to mitigate the risk of confusion and enhances the interoperability of data across different systems and domains. In addition to the aforementioned authority control resources, we leveraged other sources like DBpedia, Wikidata, and WorldCat to provide more precise and comprehensive descriptions of our entities. By using these various resources and methodologies, we were able to create a robust, interoperable, and semantically rich representation of our data that can be effectively used and understood by both humans and machines.
